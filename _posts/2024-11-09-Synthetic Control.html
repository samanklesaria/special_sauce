Synthetic Controls in Texas Prison Data¶

This post uses a synthetic control design to study whether Texas's prison building boom in 1993 resulted in them incarcerating more prisoners than they would have if their rate of prison building had continued as normal. The analysis will roughly follow the book Causal Inference: The Mixtable, but we'll use a Bayesian workflow here instead of traditional hypothesis testing.

We'll express Texas's incarceration rates over time as a mixture of those of other states. Then, we'll use this mixture of other states to construct a "synthetic" counterfactual Texas which, like the states in the mixture, didn't have a prison building boom in '93. We can compare the difference between the true and synthetic Texas over time.

We'll chose our mixture weights to combine other states into a synthetic Texas with matching poverty, alcoholism and incarceration rates during the period before 1993. $X$ will be a $S \times T \times K$ matrix where $S$ is the number of states (other than Texas), $T$ is the number of observed pre-treatment years and $K$ is the number of covariates to be matched.

To construct $X$, we will use a convenience function that converts a dataframe to a multidimensional array for which every dimension but the last enumerates possible values of a different covariate.

In [45]:
def grouped_array(df, mask, covariates, groups):
    filtered = df.loc[mask, groups + covariates]
    grouped = filtered.groupby(groups).mean()
    dims = [len(pd.unique(filtered[g])) for g in groups]
    return jnp.array(np.reshape(grouped.values, [*dims, -1])), grouped.index
